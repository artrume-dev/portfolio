Prompts ● Critical

Written instructions that give the AI its identity, brand voice, and scope — provided before any customer conversation begins.

What it is
A "system prompt" is a block of written instructions placed at the start of every session. It tells the AI who it works for, what products to focus on, how to format answers, what tone to use, and what topics are out of bounds. The customer never sees this — it runs silently in the background of every single exchange.
What prompts control
Brand voice and personality ("friendly but professional")
Which product categories the AI is allowed to discuss
Answer format — short vs detailed, with or without bullet points
Fallback behaviour when the AI doesn't know an answer
📋
Everyday Analogy
"Like the briefing document you give a new employee on their first day — it defines who they're working for, how to behave, and what they're not allowed to say."

Function Call ● Critical

The mechanism that lets the AI trigger real actions in your systems — checking stock, searching products, updating a cart — on the customer's behalf.

What it is
When the AI decides it needs to do something (not just say something), it issues a "function call" — a pre-defined action like "search products", "check inventory", or "add to cart". The AI doesn't perform these actions itself; it calls your existing systems via an API, receives the result back, and uses that real data to compose an accurate response.
Actions it can trigger
Search the product catalog for matching items
Check real-time stock availability
Add a recommended product to the customer's cart
Retrieve live pricing or active promotions
🖥️
Everyday Analogy
"Like a smart assistant pressing buttons in your internal systems on the customer's behalf — but only the buttons you've explicitly authorised it to use."

Agent Core

An AI that can plan and carry out multi-step tasks independently — without needing a human to guide each individual step.

What it is
An Agent is an AI that can break a goal into steps and execute them in sequence. Given "help me find and buy hiking boots", an agent will search products, filter by budget, compare options, present recommendations, and offer to add to cart — each step building on the last, adapting based on what it finds along the way.
Example multi-step flow
Step 1: Search catalog for "waterproof hiking boots"
Step 2: Filter results under customer's stated budget
Step 3: Compare top 3 matches across key attributes
Step 4: Present recommendation and offer to add to cart
🧑‍💼
Everyday Analogy
"Like a capable employee who doesn't need hand-holding. You give them a goal, they figure out the steps, do the work, and report back with a result."

Multi-Agent Phase 3

Multiple AI agents working in parallel — each researching a different area simultaneously — for faster, more thorough answers to complex requests.

What it is
Instead of one Agent working sequentially through a task, Multi-agent systems deploy several specialised AI agents simultaneously — each focused on their own area. One agent researches outdoor clothing, another checks footwear, a third looks at accessories. They work in parallel and pool their findings, delivering a more comprehensive answer much faster than a single agent could.
When it's most useful
Searching across multiple product categories at once
Complex "build a complete kit" or "outfit me for X" requests
High-consideration purchases needing thorough research
Scenarios where sequential search would be too slow
👥
Everyday Analogy
"Like sending a whole research team into a library at once — each person covers a different section simultaneously, and they all report back together with their findings."

Embeddings ● Critical

Converting every product's meaning into a unique mathematical fingerprint — enabling search by concept, not just by exact keywords.

What it is
An embedding model reads the full text of every product (name, description, specs, reviews, tags) and converts it into a list of numbers that captures its meaning. Two products with similar meanings will have similar number patterns — even if they use completely different words. This lets the AI match "something cosy for winter evenings" to a fleece blanket without the word "fleece" appearing anywhere in the query.
Why this matters
"Waterproof hiking boots" matches "trail shoes for wet weather" with no keyword overlap
Customer intent is understood even when phrased unusually
Your entire catalog becomes semantically searchable
Results improve as descriptions get richer
🌐
Everyday Analogy
"Like translating every product into a universal language of meaning — so the AI can compare and match products even when the words used are completely different."

Vector Database ● Critical

The intelligent library where all product fingerprints are stored and searched in milliseconds when a customer asks a question.

What it is
After Embeddings converts each product into a fingerprint, those fingerprints need somewhere to live. A Vector Database stores all of them and allows near-instant similarity search: "given this query's fingerprint, find the products whose fingerprints are most similar." It can do this across tens of thousands — or millions — of products in under a second, returning ranked results by semantic relevance.
The search process, step by step
Customer query is converted into a fingerprint (via Embeddings)
Vector DB compares that fingerprint to all stored products
Returns top matches ranked by semantic closeness
Results are fed into the RAG pipeline for response generation
📚
Everyday Analogy
"Like a super-smart library where books are shelved by meaning — so 'adventure footwear' and 'hiking shoes' end up on the same shelf, even though the words are different."

RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation ● Critical

The process that stops the AI from making things up — grounding every answer in real retrieved product data before a single word of response is written.

What it is
RAG is a two-phase process: first Retrieve, then Generate. When a customer asks a question, the system first searches your product database for relevant information. That real product data is then injected into the AI's working context so that when it writes a response, it's building on verified facts — not guessing from general training data that could be outdated, incomplete, or simply wrong.
The 4-step RAG flow
Receive: "What's the best waterproof jacket under £150?"
Retrieve: Vector DB returns 5 matching products with real specs and prices
Augment: Product data is added to the AI's context window
Generate: AI writes an accurate, grounded recommendation
📰
Everyday Analogy
"Like a journalist who looks up verified facts before writing a story — rather than writing from memory and risking getting the details wrong."

Framework Core

The software "operations manager" that connects every piece of the AI pipeline — ensuring all components work together reliably, in the right sequence, every time.

What it is
An AI framework (LangChain, LlamaIndex, Haystack) provides pre-built components and wiring patterns for connecting all parts of the system: routing queries to the right retrieval step, managing conversation memory so the AI remembers earlier messages, handling API connections to your systems, catching errors, and logging activity for monitoring and debugging.
What the framework manages
Conversation memory — AI remembers context across the whole session
Routing — which retrieval method to use for each query type
API connections to product, cart, and inventory systems
Error handling, retries, and activity logging
🍽️
Everyday Analogy
"Like a kitchen pass in a busy restaurant — it coordinates every station so that dishes arrive correctly and on time, even when dozens of orders are in flight simultaneously."

Guardrails ● Critical

Automated filters that silently check every AI response before it reaches a customer — catching hallucinated prices, off-brand language, and harmful outputs.

What it is
Guardrails are rules and filters applied to the AI's output before it's visible to anyone. They check: is this price real? Is this response on-brand? Does it contain anything misleading, harmful, or legally problematic? If a guardrail is triggered, the response is blocked and replaced with a safe fallback message. The customer never sees the problem — only the correct, safe output.
What guardrails catch
Hallucinated product prices or specifications
Responses about competitor products (if out of scope)
Off-brand tone, inappropriate language, or harmful content
Regulatory violations in sensitive categories (pharma, finance)
✍️
Everyday Analogy
"Like a legal and compliance team that reads every outgoing communication before it's sent — automatically, in real time, at scale, without slowing anything down."

Red-Teaming Core

Deliberately trying to break the AI before launch — using adversarial questions and edge cases to surface weaknesses before real customers encounter them.

What it is
Red-teaming is a pre-launch stress-testing process where a team (humans, automated tools, or both) deliberately attacks the AI with difficult, misleading, or boundary-pushing inputs. The goal is to surface problems — wrong answers, dangerous outputs, or knowledge gaps — in a controlled environment before any real customer encounters them. Findings directly improve guardrails and prompts.
What red-teamers test
"Tell me a competitor's product is better than yours"
Questions designed to make the AI hallucinate prices or specs
Edge cases: products not in the catalog, ambiguous queries
Attempts to elicit harmful, misleading, or off-brand responses
🚒
Everyday Analogy
"Like a fire drill — you'd rather discover problems in a controlled exercise than during a real incident in front of customers."

Interpretability Phase 2

Tools that make the AI's reasoning visible — so your team can understand exactly why it made a recommendation and create an accountable audit trail.

What it is
Most AI systems are "black boxes" — they produce an answer but don't explain why. Interpretability tools add a visible reasoning trail: which product data was retrieved, what factors influenced the recommendation, and what logic path led to the final answer. This is critical for regulated industries (healthcare, finance, insurance) where brands must be able to justify every suggestion made to a customer.
What it enables
Full audit trail: "This product was recommended because of X data"
Regulatory compliance in pharma, finance, and insurance
Debugging: understand why the AI made an unexpected choice
Customer trust: transparent explanations build confidence
🔍
Everyday Analogy
"Like a doctor who documents exactly why they prescribed a treatment — based on which tests, which criteria, which clinical evidence. The paper trail is the accountability."

C5 · Models — All Four Elements

Choosing the right AI brain for each job

Each model type serves a distinct purpose. A well-designed system uses all four — routing each task to the most appropriate model rather than using one model for everything.

Lg
LLM · Critical · Row 1 Primitive
Core Language Model
The primary AI brain — understands complex language, reasons through problems, and generates natural, coherent responses. Every customer conversation flows through this. Without it, nothing else works.

When to use: All standard customer conversations and product questions.
Tools: Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5
Mm
Multimodal · Phase 2 · Row 2 Composition
Image-Aware AI
Understands both text and images simultaneously. Enables visual product search: a customer photographs something they like and the AI finds similar items in your catalog — without them needing to describe it in words.

When to use: Photo-based "find something like this" product queries.
Tools: GPT-4o Vision, Claude 3, Gemini
Sm
Small Model · Phase 2 · Row 3 Deployment
Fast Pre-Filter AI
A lightweight, low-cost AI that handles simple tasks extremely quickly — like classifying query intent before routing to the main model. Saves significant cost and response time on high-volume, simple interactions.

When to use: High-volume intent classification and simple pre-filtering.
Tools: Mistral 7B, Phi-3, Gemma
Th
Thinking Model · Phase 3 · Row 4 Emerging
Deep Reasoning AI
Advanced models that reason step-by-step through complex problems before generating a response. Used for comparing products across 50+ attributes, high-consideration purchases, or queries where precision and depth matter more than speed.

When to use: Complex comparisons, specialist advice, regulated categories.
Tools: Claude 3.7 Extended Thinking, o3
